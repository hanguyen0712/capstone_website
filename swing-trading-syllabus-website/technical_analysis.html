Basic Concepts

Technical analysis is a method of identifying short-term and medium-term trading opportunities using statistical trends in share price and volume (Hayes, 2020). Technical analysis forms the foundation of swing trading. 

Technical analysis operates based on two assumptions:

  • The past performance of a stock can be indicative of its future performance due to patterns caused by market sentiment (Hayes, 2020) and other statistical processes, such as reversion around the mean. This assumption is the foundation of two prominent swing trading techniques, which are momentum trading (assuming that rising stocks will continue to rise in an uptrend) and mean reversion trading (assuming that stock prices reverse around their mean).

  • A company’s stock price is indicative of every information publicly available on the market (Hayes, 2020). Hence, there is no need to further analyze its financial information or long-term strategies. 

Below are the most common terms in technical analysis. The selection of these terms is based on IG’s “The basics of technical analysis” course. 

  1. Chart types

It is crucial for any swing trader to understand the information conveyed in three common types of stock chart: line charts, HLOC charts, and candlestick charts.

  1. Line chart 

A line chart shows the change in the stock’s closing price within a certain time period. Below is a line chart showing Tesla’s price movement from 2017 to 2020.

  1. HLOC chart (short for High – Low – Open – Close) 

A HLOC chart also shows a stock’s price movement within a certain time period, but with more information. Daily price is illustrated as a stick whose two ends represent the highest and lowest price it reached during the day. The two horizontal bars show their open and close price. The color of the bar shows the direction of the price movement that day. Red means the price decreased during the day, and green means it increased. Depending on the color of the bar, the position of the high and low points and the open and close points can switch places. For example: In the chart below, the Jan 26 stick is green, which means the lower end is the lowest price, the lower horizontal bar is the opening price, the higher horizontal bar is the closing price, and the upper end is the highest price.

  1. Candlestick chart 

A candlestick chart conveys the same information as the HLOC chart but in a different form. The two ends of the stick still represent the lowest and highest price, while the two horizontal edges of the candle represent the open and closing prices. 

  1. Support and resistance

Support and resistance can be considered the ceiling and floor prices of your stock during a given time period. When there are more buyers than sellers of a stock in the market, the stock price tends to rise to reflect the increased demand. On the contrary, when there are more sellers than buyers, the stock price falls to reflect increased supply. The support and resistance can be considered price levels where supply equals demand and the price trend reverses.

Figure 12. Toyota's support and resistance from 08/07/20,20 to 11/06/2020 (Source: Yahoo Finance, 2021)

Take Toyota’s stock from 08/07/2020 to 11/06/2020, for example. In the beginning, the price was high, so there were more people wanting to sell their shares to finalize profits than those who wanted to buy. With more supply than demand, the price dropped. The support, which is the purple line around $130, represents the price point where there were as many sellers as there were buyers of the stock in the market. Should the price drop any further, the sellers would be unwilling to sell their shares. With a decreased selling motivation and an increased buying motivation (since the stock was cheap), the supply-demand balance tilted towards the other direction and pushed the price up, until it reached the resistance (the yellow line around 137.20), where the price was high enough to discourage potential buyers from buying. 

We usually identify the support and resistance by looking at the graph and identifying price points where the stock price has repeatedly touched but was unable to breakthrough. As we zoom in or out into different time frames, our support and resistance might differ. As a rule of thumb, the more times the price touches a support (or resistance) but is unable to break through, the stronger that support (or resistance) is. 

  1. Trends

A trend is the direction that a stock is moving in. There are three types of trend: uptrend, downtrend, and sideway trend. 

Uptrends are periods of continual higher highs and higher lows (Mitchell, 2019). In contrast, downtrends are characterized by continual lower highs and lower lows. Take the Facebook stock in 2018 for example. 2018/03 – 2018/07 experienced an uptrend, with continuous higher highs and higher lows. In contrast, 2018/07 – 2018/09 experienced a downtrend, with continuous lower highs and lower lows.

The ‘scandal’ that caused the dip in 2018/07 turned out to be the release of 2nd-quarter financial reports, which showed that Facebook missed its earning target (Rodriguez, 2018).  

There is no rule of thumb to determine which horizon is long enough to characterize an uptrend or downtrend. Some people think a week of bullish movements is good enough for them, while others wait for longer. However, as illustrated in the Facebook earnings call above, traders might want to be wary around ‘crucial’ dates, such as earnings calls, product releases, court hearings, etc. since there might be sudden price movements that reverse an ongoing trend.

    During sideway trends, the stock does not move in a distinct direction. You can think of sideway trends as “flat horizontal” periods in the stock chart.
