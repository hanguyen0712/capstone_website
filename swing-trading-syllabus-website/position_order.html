1. Position

A position is an ownership or short sale (a.k.a. selling shares they do not yet own and hoping to buy them later at a lower price) of a security. A long position means purchasing and owning a stock hoping that its price will rise later, and the shares can be sold for a profit. A short position means selling shares that we do not yet own, hoping the price will decrease in the future so we can buy back at a profit. Investors taking a long position are often bullish about the stock (a.k.a. they expect the price to go up), while those taking a short position are bearish about it (a.k.a. they expect the price to go down).

Opening a position means purchasing (or short selling) a stock. Closing/Exiting a position means selling it (or buying it back). Positions can be closed voluntarily (by investors) or involuntarily (by brokerages). For example, if investors place a stop-loss order and the stock price has fallen to the stop price, the order will be automatically converted to a market order and executed, closing their position per their initial instruction. The return of an investment can only be calculated after the position is closed. 

  1. Order

An order is how you state your request of buying or selling a security. It instructs your brokerage/trading platform on how to execute or cancel your order accordingly. Using the right type of order minimizes your losses during downturns and maximizes your profits during an upside, hence it is considered a basic yet effective risk mitigation technique. Here are the most common order types:

  • Market order: buy/sell a security immediately at roughly the current price.

  • Limit buy/sell order: buy/sell a security if it reaches a pre-specified or lower/higher price. This pre-specified price should be lower/higher than the current price. Limit orders help to secure our profits.

  • Buy/sell stop (or stop loss) order: buy/sell a security if it reaches a pre-specified price that is higher/lower than the current price. Once this pre-specified price (called a stop price) is reached, the order is converted to a market or limit order. Stop orders help to limit our losses.

  • Stop-limit order: A combination of a stop order and a limit order. This order requires setting two prices: a stop price, below which the order is converted to a market sell order, and a limit price, above which the order is converted to a limit sell order. This order helps us both minimize our losses in a downturn while not limiting our profits if the price increases.

  • Take-profit order: similar to a limit order, but the order is executed if the price reaches the exact pre-specified price. The disadvantage of this order is that investors cannot enjoy the additional returns if the stock price rises above their pre-specified price. Its advantage is that it helps prevent possible future losses due to unexpected downturns.

  • All or none (AON) order: unless the entire size of the order (e.g., 10,000 shares of AAPL) is filled, this order is cancelled.

  • Immediate or cancel (IOC) order: the order must be filled, even partially, immediately. If none of the order is filled immediately, it is cancelled.

  • Fill or kill (FOK) order: a combination of the AON and IOC orders. Unless the order is completely filled immediately, it is cancelled.

  • Good ‘til canceled (GTC) order: the order is active until you cancel it, or until the maximum time period that the brokerage allows an order to be active is reached.

  • Day order: if the order is not executed during the trading day, it is cancelled.
