Swing trading 101

  1. What is swing trading?

Swing trading is a stock trading practice that attempts to profit off ‘uptrends’ and ‘downtrends’ in the market. The investment horizon can last anywhere from several days to several months, depending on how long the trend lasts and how well investors can capitalize on it.

  1. Arbitrage

Arbitrage is a crucial concept in swing trading. It assumes that the market, in the short run, is inefficient. Therefore, the same asset or security might be priced differently in different the market and during different periods. Swing traders attempt to buy shares when stock prices are low and sell when they rise later, profiting off of the price differences.

  1. Trade vs. invest

Investing and trading are often mistakenly used interchangeably in the market. Therefore, it is worth clarifying the differences between them up-front to students.

Investing is a long-term activity where investors are spared from the short-term and even mid-term fluctuations of the market. In exchange, they need to conduct a thorough analysis of their portfolio companies’ long-term prospects to be reasonably confident that their investments will be profitable down the line. The holding horizon for investing is typically years.

In contrast, trading means traders profit off the short- and mid-term fluctuations. Shares are sold once they turn a profit, and the holding horizon ranges anywhere from a few minutes (in intraday trading) to a few months. Traders have to care about anything that can remotely impact the stock price in an attempt to “beat the market” and earn money from price arbitrage. 

Though they seem similar, the mindsets required for these two activities are very different. 

  1. Swing trading pros and cons

There are three main advantages to swing trading. First, since the holding horizon is short (ranging from a few days to a few months), the capital does not get tied down for a significant time (Samuelsson, 2021). Thus, there is less opportunity cost associated with spending money on each trade. Second, swing trading can be an additional income source beside one’s primary income. Third, since swing trading attempts to profit from price trends and fluctuations, there is not much pressure to research companies’ fundamentals and long-term growth prospects as rigorously as in long-term investing. Let us take a look at GameStop (GME) to see how a swing trader might make profits even from underperforming stocks. Traditionally, GME’s price has never exceeded $50 per share. Recent internal changes in the company’s operation (e.g., the announcement of three new board members), as well as external market conditions, are also not indicative of significant price fluctuations.  

However, at the end of 2020, a group of traders on Reddit side r/WallStreetBets rallied to make GME a “meme stock.” These traders cheered one another on to buy and hold as many shares as possible, eventually creating a “short squeeze” that forced the price to increase by over 1,000% in late January. After the squeeze, the share price rapidly plummeted.

 

A swing trader can make substantial profits from the stock if they buy shares any time before Jan 24 and sell any time before Jan 31.  

However, there are also several downsides associated with swing trading. First, extrapolating from the GameStop example above, any trader buying shares near the Jan 28 peak and holding should lose a sizable sum of money. Traders attempt to predict near-future trends in the market, but any attempt to predict the future is only speculative and subject to failure. Second, since swing traders do not typically research companies’ fundamentals very thoroughly and do not hold their trade for long, they might miss out on “unicorns” that return exceptional profits in the long run. Take Tesla (TSLA), for example. It is reasonable for swing traders buying shares before Jan 2020 to cash out during its price surge in Feb 2020 since the profit margin should be good enough for them. However, investors who held the stock for longer would enjoy much better returns, as share price increased many folds during the rest of 2020. 

In conclusion, swing trading is no miraculous get-rich-quick scheme. It has just as many pros and cons as other trading styles. Therefore, swing traders must understand the risks before engaging in swing trading activities and employ appropriate risk mitigation measures to secure their profits.
