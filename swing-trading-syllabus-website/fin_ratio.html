Now that we have covered the basics of financial statements, it is time to analyze them and compare them to the industry average. Understandably, financial ratios can be a bit difficult to grasp, which is why I have decided to introduce them in the context of a case study to illustrate the concepts better. 

    Surely, a majority of investors and traders have heard about Toyota and Tesla, two global giants by market valuation. Toyota stock has been in the market for decades, while Tesla stock has continually made headlines with their rapid price rise and an infamous 5:1 split in 2020 (Tesla, 2020). If you are a new trader looking to buy your first stocks in the automobile industry and are considering these two, read on for a thorough analysis of their financial statements!

About the companies

Toyota

           Toyota is a Japanese automobile company founded in 1937. Its current CEO is Akio Toyoda, the great-grandson of founder Kiichiro Toyoda, a charismatic leader who strongly emphasizes humility, personal responsibility, and public service. The company operates based on such principles as "be faithful to your duties," "be studious and creative," and "be practical and avoid frivolousness" (Toyota, n.d). These guiding philosophies translate into their production system as well. The "Toyota Production System" (TPS), which emphasizes "kaizen" (continuous improvement), "jidoka" (stopping production/operation completely when there is an error to ensure safety), and Just-in-Time (only produce the exact amount needed of what is required when it is needed to prevent waste), is internationally renowned as the gold standard of lean manufacturing (Toyota, n.d.). Nouns commonly associated with the company include “reliability,” “safety”, and “legacy”. 

Toyota defines itself as an engineering company (Toyota Times, 2020). The company produces a ,”ide variety of passenger cars, SUVs, and industrial equipment whose engines run on gasoline, diesel, or a hybrid of the aforementioned and electricity (Toyota Industries Corporation, n.d.). Consistent with its vision of 'Creating Mobility for All' (Toyota, n.d.), the cars are marketed under five different brands (Toyota, Hino, Lexus, Ranz, Daihatsu) (Wikipedia, n.d.) to accommodate customers of various social backgrounds and locomotive needs. In 2021, the company releases three new electric SUVs for the US market (Toyota, 2021), a move in line with the current global shift to environmentally-friendly vehicles. 

Toyota's primary markets include North America (over 30% units sold consistently from 2015 to 2020), Japan (roughly 25%), Europe (approximately 10%), and Asia (about 15%) (Toyota, 2020). It was the most valuable auto company in the world until Tesla surpassed its market value in 2020 (Stevens, 2020).

Tesla

Tesla is a US automobile company founded in 2003 in San Carlos, California, by Elon Musk, a visionary leader noted with bold investment decisions and a creative and idealistic leadership style (Wingard, 2019). Such is his risk-taking tendency and eccentricity that he once claimed that he gave Tesla a less than 10% success rate when he started the company (Huddleston, 2018). Adjectives commonly associated with the company and their founder are “innovative”, “disruptive,” and “trendy”. 

The company produces high-tech hybrid and electric cars, solar roofs and panels, and apparel. Although Tesla was not profitable until 2020 (Davies, 2020) and so many people believed it would fail that it became the most shorted stock in the US (Reinicke, 2020). Yet, its stock price continued to rise. By January 2020, its market capitalization has surpassed Volkswagen, Ford, and GM, making it the second most valuable auto company in the world (Shipley, 2020). By July 2020, its market valuation surpassed that of Toyota (Stevens, 2020), making it the most valuable auto company globally. 

Headquartered at Silicon Valley's heart, the company operates more like a tech company than a traditional automobile maker (Shipley, 2020). They place a strong emphasis on developing software for their cars, as shown in their commitment to building self-driving technology. New updates are released regularly (Shipley, 2020), upgrading users' cars the same way their phones are upgraded. Furthermore, instead of running advertisements and setting up a network of dealerships like traditional automakers, Tesla sells their cars directly online to consumers (Shipley, 2020). This significantly simplifies the selling-buying process and reduces transaction costs.

    Trends
  • Revenue

From 2017 to 2020, Toyota saw decreasing revenue growth. The company went from enjoying a 12% growth in 2018 to a negative growth the following year. In 2020, the company only logged a 2% revenue increase. Although this figure is higher than the industry average of 0.90% (Damodaran, 2020), the difference seems insignificant. The negative growth in 2019 and the subsequent slow growth was probably caused by Toyota’s recall of several Prius hybrids over faulty equipment (Bomey, 2019), which damaged their sales and reputation as a reliable car manufacturer. However, the company seemed to have regained their reputation for producing safe and reliable cars in the following year (Hirsch, 2021), so there is hope that their revenue will improve. 

In contrast, in the past four years, Tesla has seen significant double-figure revenue increases. In 2020, the company saw a 15% revenue growth, which was almost 17 times the industry average. 

In conclusion, while Toyota experienced slowed down revenue growth, Tesla enjoyed sustained and exceptionally strong growth. 

  • EBIT:

To evaluate Toyota’s earnings trend, I use EBIT instead of net income because the former allows me to analyze their operational performance without the impact of M&As & subsidiaries. Toyota’s EBIT growth has been unstable in the previous three years, with the company logging a 25% EBIT increase in 2018 only to witness a significant 16% decrease the following year. In 2020, Toyota saw a 15% EBIT increase. The slump in EBIT growth was likely caused by Toyota’s 2019 Prius recall scandal, which damaged their profitability and reputation in the market. 

On the other hand, it is not useful to analyze Tesla’s EBIT growth since the company did not have a positive EBIT until 2019. That said, the significant increase in EBIT from 2019 to 2020 might signal that the company has significantly improved their profitability in one short year. 

    Profitability ratios
  • Net margin: 

Net margin, calculated by dividing net income over revenue, measures the net profitability of a company after paying interest expenses and taxes. The higher the net margin is, the better the company is faring. 

The average margin of major car companies globally is around 3.7% (Damodaran, 2020). From 2017 to 2020, Toyota has consistently exceeded this benchmark, logging a 7% margin on average. This means that Toyota has been more profitable than the average car company globally. On the other hand, Tesla did not make a profit (had negative profit margins) until 2020. In 2020, the company saw its first positive net margin of 2%, which was lower than the industry average. However, given that Tesla is a startup, it is not surprising for it to incur losses during the first few years of operation due to the high costs of setting up operation, research and development, and customer acquisition. Given their strong revenue growth in the past four years, Tesla’s net margin will undoubtedly improve in the next years if they keep up this momentum. Another reason for us to believe that Tesla will improve its profit margin significantly in the years to come is its superiority over competitors in technology (Moorhead, 2020). As Tesla’s electronics are currently estimated to be six years ahead of Toyota and Volkswagen (Moorhead, 2020), the company will have both a competitive advantage in car sales and the potential to sell/leash their software/electronic technology as an additional income stream. 

In conclusion, Toyota currently has stable profitability above the industry average. Meanwhile, Tesla is a rising startup that has just entered the ‘making profit’ phase and is expected to be much more profitable in the years to come due to their competitive advantages. 

  • Cost of goods sold (COGS)/sales (revenue):

The COGS/Sales tells us how much money a company spends on producing their goods per dollar of revenue. The average COGS/Sales of the global car industry is 82.8% (Damodaran, 2020), which means for each dollar of revenue earned, the company has to spend 82.8 cents on production costs. In general, a lower COGS/Sales is better because the revenue is less eaten up by costs. 

Toyota’s COGS/Sales has stabilized around 82%, which is the industry average. In contrast, in 2020, Tesla brought down their COGS/Sales from 83% to 79%, lower than the industry average. This improvement in COGS/Sales is consistent with what we know about the company’s production system. First, a Tesla car has fewer parts than a traditional car (Shipley, 2020). Second, the company has been working on further reducing production costs by achieving economies of scale on their innovative battery technologies (Dans, 2020). 

In conclusion, Toyota’s COGS/Sales is around the industry average. In contrast, Tesla has been working on bringing down production costs and has succeeded so far. 

Efficiency ratios
  • Asset turnover:

Asset turnover, calculated by dividing revenue over assets, measures how much revenue a company generates relative to their assets. A higher asset turnover means the company is using their assets effectively to generate revenue. 

Toyota’s asset turnover has hovered around 0.57, which means for each dollar of assets, the company only generates 57 cents of revenue. Meanwhile, Tesla’s asset turnover from 2018 to 2020 has been at least 0.60. We can conclude that so far, Tesla has been using their assets to generate revenue more effectively than Toyota. 

  • Inventory turnover:

The inventory turnover, calculated by dividing COGS over inventory, measures how well a company manages their inventory. A low inventory turnover either means the company has not sold their goods well (hence the small nominator) or has overstocked (hence the large denominator). However, a too high inventory turnover might indicate understocking and imply that the company is missing out on sales opportunities (their supplies cannot match customers’ demands). The car industry’s average inventory turnover is 10.84 (CSI Market, 2021), which means on average, the number of cars sold during the year is over 10 times the cars stocked in inventory. 

Toyota’s inventory turnover has been consistently slightly lower than the industry average. In 2020, their inventory turnover increased to 10.07, which was approximately the industry average. In contrast, Tesla has enjoyed a much lower inventory turnover that reached a height of 6.07 in 2020. This does not necessarily mean the company is doing poorly, however, since Tesla’s method of recognizing a sale differs from traditional automakers. Specifically, the former records a sale when they deliver a car directly to their customers, while the latter records a sale when they deliver a car to dealerships (Root, 2020). Another reassuring sign is that Tesla’s inventory turnover has been improving over the years despite continual double-figure increases in their inventory, which means the company has made progress in both managing their inventory and selling their products. 

  • Return on asset (ROA):

A company’s ROA, calculated by dividing net income over total assets, measures how much net profit a company makes relative to their assets. The average ROA of the global car industry in 2020 was 1.05%.

Toyota’s ROA has stabilized around 4% over the last four years, which was higher than the industry average. In contrast, Tesla has just managed to have a positive ROA in 2020 when their net income became positive. That said, it is normal for startups to have low ROAs in the first few years of operation since they are unlikely to make any profit yet. Furthermore, Tesla’s ROA has been improving steadily over the years and reached the industry average on the first year it was positive. Given the strong increase in revenue and improvements in asset management, Tesla’s ROA has the potential to exceed the industry average in the following years. 

  • Return on equity (ROE)

The return on equity of a company, calculated by dividing net income over shareholders’ equity, measures how much profit a company makes with each dollar that investors have invested in it. The higher an ROE is, the more effectively the company turns their invested money into profit. The average ROE of the global car and truck industry in 2020 was 8.55% (Damodaran, 2020). 

Toyota’s ROE has been consistently around 10% in the past few years, which is higher than the industry average. In contrast, since Tesla’s net income was not positive until 2020, their ROE was also not positive until 2020. The company’s ROE now sits at 3%, which is lower than the industry average. However, this should not be considered a worrying sign because it is expected of Tesla, a startup, to have a negative net income in their first few years of operation. As Tesla has continuously improved their ROE over the years and given their current strong revenue growth and competitive advantages, it is reasonable to expect their ROE to increase significantly in the following years. 

Valuation ratios
  • Price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio: 

The P/E ratio of a company, calculated by dividing their share price by their earnings per share, measures how much investors value them relative to their earnings. A lower P/E value means the stock is cheaper relative to their earnings, either because investors have yet to realize their potential or because they have identified issues that will seriously hamper their future prospects. On the other hand, a too high P/E value might indicate that the stock price is overpriced (too expensive relative to their earnings). The average P/E ratio for cars and truck companies globally in 2020 was 39.73 (Damodaran, 2020). Toyota’s P/E ratio has hovered around 11 – 12, which is significantly lower than the industry average. This means Toyota’s stock is now relatively cheap to buy, given its earnings. However, the P/E ratio alone cannot give us all the information necessary to determine whether the stock is a good buy or not. Investors might value the stock lowly because they have not realized its potential (in this case, the stock is a buy) or because they have seen issues with its management/operation that will erode its future profitability. 

Tesla’s P/E ratio does not make sense because, until 2020, the company did not have a positive net income. Therefore, the P/E ratio is not an appropriate metric to evaluate the company. 

  • Price/Book ratio: 

The price/book ratio, calculated by dividing the market value of a company by its total equity, determines how much investors value a company relative to their equity. The market value is calculated as the product of the current share price and the number of shares outstanding. The price/book ratio is particularly useful when we evaluate companies with negative earnings, such as Tesla. Similar to the P/E ratio, a lower P/B ratio might mean the company is underpriced or that they have encountered serious issues that will hamper their future profitability. On the other hand, an abnormally high P/B ratio indicates that the stock price might be inflated out of proportion to their book value. The average price/book ratio of car companies worldwide in 2020 was 1.03 (Damodaran, 2020). 

From 2017 to 2020, Toyota’s P/B ratio has hovered around 1.18, which is close to the industry average. In contrast, Tesla’s P/B ratio has steadily been double-figure. Notably, in 2020, they saw a P/B ratio of 34, which is 34 times higher than the industry average. While investors are reasonably optimistic about Tesla’s prospects as a leader in the high-tech, environmentally-friendly vehicle industry, this abnormally high P/B ratio might signal that the stock is overpriced.

  • Price/sales ratio:

The price/sales ratio, calculated by dividing the market value of a company by its revenue, is a measure of how investors value a company relative to their revenue. This ratio follows similar rules of thumb to the P/B and P/E ratios: a significantly lower than average P/S ratio might signal an undervaluation or a serious operational issue, while a significantly higher than average P/S might mean an overvaluation. In 2020, the average P/S ratio in the global car industry was 0.48. Toyota’s P/S was consistently around 0.70, which was slightly higher than the industry average. This might signal that investors generally view Toyota as a better-than-average company in the car industry. However, given that they are a long-standing player in the industry with the second-largest market valuation globally and a line of trendy electric vehicles, this P/s is unimpressive.

In contrast, Tesla’s P/S ratio has always been higher than the industry average, signaling investors’ optimism in the company’s prospects. Notably, in 2020, their P/S shot up to 24.23 as they had positive net earnings for the first time. However, this abnormally higher-than-average P/S might signal that the stock is currently overpriced.

  • Retention ratio: 

The retention ratio, calculated by the formula (Earnings per share – Dividends per share)/Earnings per share, measures how much earnings a company reinvests in their operation or pays off debts. The more money a company retains, the more money they have to scale up and improve their profitability in the future. The average retention ratio of the car industry in 2020 was 54.8% (Damodaran, 2020). 

Toyota has been retaining around 70% of their earnings and paying out around 30% as dividends. Since they are a stable and mature company, it makes sense for them to pay out regular dividends. 

On the other hand, Tesla has been retaining all of their earnings. Since the company is a startup and was not profitable until 2020, it makes sense for them to focus all streams of money into setting up and scaling up their operation.

  • Sustainable growth rate (SGR):

The sustainable growth rate, calculated as the product of the retention ratio and ROE, tells us the maximum speed at which a company can grow with their current debt/equity structure. Generally, the higher the SGR is, the better. Toyota has seen their SGR hovering around 7% in the past few years, which is higher than the 2020 industry average of 5% (Damodaran, 2020). This means that given the current debt/equity structure, the company can still grow at a slightly above-average rate.

Tesla’s SGR currently does not make sense because the company did not have a positive net income until 2020 (Damodaran, 2016). Therefore, we cannot evaluate the company’s SGR at this stage. 

Liquidity
  • Current ratio

    The current ratio measures a company’s ability to fulfill short-term liabilities. Its formula is current assets/current liabilities. In theory, a higher current ratio is preferable because having more assets relative to liabilities means the company pays off short-term debts more quickly. A ratio of less than one is alarming since their asset is not enough to cover obligations and liabilities. However, on the other hand, a current ratio significantly larger than one does not necessarily mean the company is thriving either. In this case, the company might be too conservative in their financing and not expand as much as possible. They can take on more debt to scale up their operations and still be assured that their current asset is enough to pay off the debts. As a rule of thumb, a good current ratio is typically above 1.50 (Fernando, 2021). 

    From 2017 to 2020, Toyota’s current ratio stabilizes around 1.04, which means the company’s current asset is just enough to cover current liabilities. On the other hand, in 2017 and 2018, Tesla was in poor financial health with a current ratio of less than one. Their situation improved significantly in 2019 and 2020, however, with a ratio reaching a height of 1.88 in 2020. Though both companies can now pay off their short-term obligations, they the trend in current ratio changes shows us that Toyota is a more ‘stable’ company that has been in a borderline illiquid situation for years. It is reasonable for us to expect that without significant changes in market condition or the company’s strategy, Toyota’s current ratio will continue hovering at this level. In contrast, Tesla is a ‘rising star’ with rapidly improving financial health and has improved their liquidity to a ‘safe’ zone. 

  • Cash ratio

The cash ratio measures a company’s ability to immediately pay off current liabilities with cash and cash equivalents, the most liquid form of the asset they have. Its formula is cash & cash equivalents/current liabilities. Like the current ratio, a cash ratio of less than one means the company does not have enough cash to pay off current liabilities immediately. However, contrary to the current ratio, a slightly lower than one cash ratio might not pose troubles to a company as long as they are not required to immediately pay off a significant portion of their current liabilities. However, too high a cash ratio indicates that the company is not using its cash efficiently, letting its money sit instead of investing it or paying out dividends. As a rule of thumb, the cash ratio should ideally be from 0.5 to 1.0 (Corporate Finance Institute, n.d.). 

Toyota’s cash ratio has been around 0.2 throughout the years, which means the company might have trouble paying off current liabilities without liquidating more assets immediately. In contrast, Tesla’s cash ratio has been improving over the year, reaching a height of 1.36 in 2020. This means the company can easily pay off current obligations immediately without having to sell additional assets. Since Tesla is a more liquid company than Toyota, they can potentially jump on new investment opportunities more quickly should they show up because they have cash lying around ready to be used. 

Leverage ratios
  • Debt/Equity (D/E) ratio 

The D/E ratio, calculated by dividing a company’s liabilities by their shareholders’ equity, measures how much debt a company has compared to the money that their shareholders have invested in them (or “equity”). A D/E of less than one means their equity can fully pay off their debts if needed, while a significantly high D/E might be alarming because the company might not be able to pay off their debts, especially during an economic downturn. Both Toyota and Tesla have a D/E ratio of over 1.0, which is higher than the industry average of 0.53, but not significantly higher. This means although their shareholders’ equity is not enough to cover all the debt, they are unlikely to face any trouble since a good portion of the debt is long-term, as shown in their balance sheets.

  • Interest coverage ratio        

The interest coverage ratio, calculated by dividing earnings before interest and tax by interest expense, measures how many times a company can pay off their interest with their pre-tax pre-interest earnings. Generally, the higher the interest coverage ratio is, the better because the company makes enough money to cover more cycles of interest expense. An interest coverage ratio of under 1.0 is alarming because it means the company is not making enough money to cover their interest expense, let alone pay off the debt. 

Toyota’s interest coverage ratio has stayed in the eighties for the past few years, indicating their ample ability to pay off their interest. Meanwhile, Tesla has just made enough to cover over two cycles of interest expense in 2020. However, it is reasonable for a startup to have low or negative earnings in their first years of operations, so this low interest coverage ratio might not be concerning. 

Conclusion

    Toyota’s COGS/Sales, asset turnover, and inventory turnover have highlighted potential room for improvement in their asset and inventory management. Regarding liquidity, both the current ratio and cash ratio show that the company is not adequately liquid, though they are still likely to pay off their debts during an economic downturn. On the other hand, they remain a long-standing player in the global car industry with a high market valuation and stable year-to-year performance. Though their revenue and earnings growth seems to have stagnated recently due to a combination of recall scandals and a lack of disruptive strategies, it is likely that these figures will improve in the following years as the company regains their reputation in the market and releases new electric vehicle models into the US market. Furthermore, their P/E, P/B, and P/S ratios show that investors are valuing them at a discount compared to the industry average. Since their current issues with efficiency and liquidity are not alarming, they are likely to continue growing at an above-average rate, and their stock price is now relatively low. Their stock might be a buy for long-term investors. 

    On the other hand, despite encountering startups’ typical earnings-related issues, Tesla has been growing fast, improving their profitability, and increasing their asset and cost management efficiency. The company has also become more liquid, enabling them to invest more to scale up their operation. The only issue is that the stock currently seems overpriced, so the stock price might decrease to reflect the company’s valuation over the long run accurately. However, given the current earnings trend and what we know about Tesla’s competitive advantages, it is likely that Tesla will be profitable in the following years and trade in an uptrend. Thus, as of now, Tesla seems more suitable to be a pick for short-to-medium term trading.
