Financial securities are financial assets that you can trade in the open market. These include equity securities, debt securities, derivative securities, and hybrid securities. 

  1. Equity securities

Equity securities are investments in exchange for a portion of ownership of a public company. These include common stock and preferred stock. 

Although some companies pay out regular dividends, holders of equity securities mainly profit from capital gains when they sell their shares. These capital gains are not promised, however, since the stock price might decrease when the holder wants to sell it. Furthermore, if the company goes bankrupt, shareholders are the last in line to be paid (after all other creditors, such as bondholders). This payout might sometimes amount to nothing. 

On the other hand, equity security holders get to vote on companies’ decisions (e.g., change of leadership, major business expansion plans, etc.) based on a pro-rata basis. Pro-rata simply means that their vote’s weight is proportional to the number of shares they hold. 

It is worth noting that “stocks” and “shares” are two different terms. A “stock” refers to ownership of companies in general. If a person says, “I bought 50 stocks”, it means they have partial ownership of 50 different companies. In contrast, a “share” refers to ownership of a specific one. For example, we can say, “I own 50 shares of Facebook.”

  1. Debt securities

Debt securities are essentially loans. They include bonds and certificates of deposit, among a few other debt instruments. When investors lend out money to companies or governments, they also agree on an interest rate, interest payment frequency, and a maturity date when their investment is returned to them. Debt security holders do not have voting rights. Just like bank loans, the interest rate is determined by the perceived ‘risk’ of the borrower – the less likely the borrower seems to be able to pay off the debt, the higher the interest rate. Also, similar to bank loans, debt securities can either be backed by collateral (secured) or not. 

Debt securities are generally safer than equity securities due to the fixed expected returns and maturity date. Fun fact: the U.S. Treasury bonds are so safe they are considered risk-free (a.k.a. the debts are almost 100% guaranteed to be repaid). Their interest rate is thus often used as the risk-free rate of return (a.k.a. the rate of return one would expect from a zero-risk investment during a certain time period) in the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). 

Due to the scope of this website, we will not be discussing this type of securities. Students can learn more about debt securities here.

  1. Derivative securities

Derivative securities derive their value from other instruments, including stocks, bonds, commodities, indices, and currencies. There are four main types of derivative securities: Options, Forwards, Futures, and Swaps.

  1. Options

An option contract gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset at a pre-determined price by a pre-determined date in the future. Buying a call option gives you the right to buy, while buying a put option gives you the right to sell. Using options can be a good risk-hedging technique, which students can learn more about in the risk mitigation section.

  1. Forwards

A forward contract is a customized agreement between two parties regarding the buying or selling of an asset (most likely commodities, such as gold, grains, or oil) at a pre-determined price at a pre-determined date in the future. Forward contracts are not available on exchanges. Students can find out more about forward contracts here.

  1. Futures

A future contract is a standardized agreement between two parties regarding the buying or selling of an asset at a pre-determined price at a pre-determined date in the future. Future contracts are available on exchanges. Students can find out more about future contracts here.

  1.  Swaps

A swap contract is an agreement between two parties to exchange cash flows or liabilities. Swaps are not available on exchanges. Students can find out more about swap contracts here.  

  1. Hybrid securities

Hybrid securities are a crossbreed of debt and equity securities. These include convertible bonds, preference shares, among other instruments. Due to the scope of this website, we will not be discussing this type of securities. Students can learn more about hybrid securities here.
