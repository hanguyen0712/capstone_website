Financial statements are legal documents that companies are required to file at the end of each quarter or year to report on their performance to the government and the public. A company’s financial statements are the most concrete and legitimate evidence of its condition. As investors/traders, we usually look at three types of financial statements: the balance sheet, the income statement, and the cash flow statement. 

  1. Balance sheet

The balance sheet shows the assets, liabilities, and stakeholders’ equity of a company at a particular date, most commonly the end of a financial year. The assets section shows what the company is currently possessing or is expected to possess. The liabilities section shows the debts and other responsibilities that the company has to pay off or fulfill. The stakeholders’ equity section shows the money the company has left to distribute among stockholders or reinvest in the business after all liabilities are fulfilled. As an accounting principle, the total assets must equal the sum of total liabilities and stakeholders’ equity.

    Items on the balance sheet include:

  • Cash and cash equivalent: the amount of cash and other liquid assets the company possesses.

  • Marketable securities: assets that the company can sell for cash quickly (e.g., stock, bonds).

  • Accounts receivable: money that the company will be paid in the near future. For example, if they have sold goods to their partners and the partners have promised to pay them within 90 days, the proceeds from that transaction are considered an accounts receivable.  

  • Inventories: raw materials, unfinished products, finished products ready for sale.

  • Current assets: assets that the company can use (and expects to use) over the next year. These include cash, marketable securities, account receivables, inventories, among other items. 

  • Property, plant, and equipment (PP&E): assets including factories, machinery, land, and office blocks that the company owns. 

  • Short-term borrowings: debts that the company must pay off in the short run (e.g., a few months). 

  • Accounts payable: goods and services that the company has used but has not paid for and is expected to pay off soon. 

  • Income taxes payable: the amount of tax the company is due to pay off.

  • Current liabilities: debts and other obligations that the company must pay off in the short run (e.g., a few months). 

  • Long-term liabilities: debts and other obligations that the company can fulfill in a few years or longer. 

  • Mezzanine equity: a hybrid of debt and equity.

  • Common stocks: the total book value of shares owned by common shareowners.

  • Retained earnings: the amount of money that the company reinvests in its operation or pays off debts.

  • Treasury stocks: shares that the company has repurchased from the market. 

  1. Cash flow statement

The cash flow statement tells us how the company spends and earns their money over a period of time, most commonly a financial year. The cash flow statement has three main components:

  • Cash provided by operating activities: money spent or earned from day-to-day business (e.g., from the production and sale of goods and services).

  • Cash used in investing activities: money spent on long-term investments and PP&E.

  • Cash used in financing activities: money spent on paying dividends to shareholders and repurchasing stocks, and money earned by issuing bonds and shares.

  1. Income statement

The income statement shows the profitability of a company over a period of time, most commonly a financial year. 

Items on the income statement include:

  • Revenue: the total amount of money earned from selling goods and services.

  • Cost of products sold: the amount of money spent on producing the goods and services sold (e.g., raw materials to produce goods, the hourly wage for employees)

  • Selling, general and administrative: Any costs that do not directly contribute to the production of goods and services. These include salary paid for the management team, legal expenses, and marketing expenses.

  • Operating income: the company’s income before paying off interest expenses and taxes.

  • Provision for income taxes: the amount of taxes that the company expects to pay

  • Net income: the company’s final profitability after subtracting interest expenses and taxes.

    Usually, we would want the net income to be as high as possible. It would be even better if over the years, the net income continually increases. As a measure of profitability, net income usually has a direct impact on share price. The higher the net income (especially when it beats analysts and investors’ expectations), the higher the share price.

Although a negative net income means the company is spending more money than it earns (hence incurs a loss), this is not necessarily a bad sign. Startups on their first years of operations tend to burn more cash than they earn due to high set-up, investment, and customer acquisition costs. If these startups are truly good, their income will turn from negative to positive in the future, bringing profitability to investors. Otherwise, they might go bankrupt and render their shares worthless.
