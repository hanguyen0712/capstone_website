After learning about the main types of financial security, students should benefit from learning about the environment where trading stocks in particular, and financial securities in general, occur: financial markets.

  1. What is a financial market?

A financial market is where investors and businesses meet to buy and sell financial securities. For example, as an individual entering the stock market, you are an investor seeking to buy stocks, whereas the companies whose shares you buy sell them.

  1. Types of financial markets

There are four main types of financial markets: 

  • The stock market, where we trade shares of stocks. The stock market is made up of many stock exchanges around the world, including the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), the Tokyo Stock Exchange (TSE), and the London Stock Exchange (LSE).

  • The bond market, where we trade debt securities. Companies and governments sell bonds to raise capital, and investors buy bonds to receive their money plus a fixed interest back after a pre-specified period. Due to the scope of this website, we are not discussing this concept thoroughly. Students can find out more about the bond market here.

  • The derivatives market, where we trade financial derivatives such as futures and options. Due to the scope of this website, we are not discussing this concept thoroughly. Students can find out more about the derivatives market here.

  • The commodities market, where we trade raw products (e.g., crude oil, coffee, gold). As individual investors, however, you most likely buy stocks, mutual funds, index funds, exchange-traded funds (ETFs), or futures contracts of companies involved in these commodities instead. Due to the scope of this website, we are not discussing this concept thoroughly. Students can find out more about the commodity market here.

For the purpose of this website, we only focus on the stock market from now on. The term ‘the market’ now refers to ‘the stock market.’

  1. Why do we need financial markets?

This section addresses why financial markets exist in the first place. Three main reasons for their existence are: 

  • They provide companies and governments with additional access to capital (e.g., besides institutional lenders). On the other hand, individual investors have more opportunities to make money. As investing in the stock market is riskier than putting money in a savings account (learn more about risk in the risk and volatility section), the former presumably has higher returns than the latter. 

  • They provide transparency to the market. All investors, and borrowers, are treated equally according to a set of predefined rules no matter their size. Furthermore, theoretically, all investors get easy access to the same information (e.g., financial statements, M&A news, change of leadership announcements) to inform their decisions. Besides, since everyone trades in the same market, prices are often standardized. There are few arbitrage opportunities (e.g., buying a stock on the LSE and selling it on the NYSE because the former’s listed price is notably lower than on the latter.)

  • They increase the liquidity of financial assets and lower transaction costs. Sellers do not have to worry about finding buyers and vice versa - they can trade securities any time they want. Due to this ease of access, transaction costs are minimal. Some platforms, like Robinhood, even allow commission-free trading, which means investors buy and sell securities for free.

  1. Bull vs. bear market

“Bull” and “bear” are the two states of a financial market. A bull market means the economy is doing great and, in general, generating increasing returns for investors. In a bull market, the price of a majority of stocks reaching higher highs and higher lows (a.k.a. and uptrend). Investors in a bull market are often optimistic about the prospects of making profits. The U.S. market in 2019 was bullish, characterized by an over 30% increase in the S&P500 value.

Figure 1. Bullish U.S. market in 2019 as shown by the movement of the S&P500.

In contrast, a bear market means the economy is showing signs of slowing down/recession and generating diminished returns for investors. A general rule of thumb is that if a market declines by at least 20% from its most recent peak, it is considered bearish. For example, from February to March 2020, the S&P500 decreased by around 34% due to the global impact of COVID-19, undoing the progress of the entire previous year. Investors in a bear market expect that the prices will drop further. It is more difficult to trade in a bear market: buying securities at any point means running the risk of taking a loss since the possibility of prices decreasing is greater than that of them increasing. Investors instead opt for strategies such as short selling (explained in the position and order section) or buying put options (explained in the risk mitigation section).
