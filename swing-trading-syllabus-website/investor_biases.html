As human beings, each of our decisions is subject to cognitive and emotional biases, which might have detrimental consequences when we trade in the market. In this section, three common biases and how to address them will be introduced. Of course, there are many other biases that might impact your investment decision. Students can check out this article on cognitive biases and this article on cognitive and emotional biases if they want to learn more! 

  1. Overconfidence bias

There is a popular research finding that most people think they are more intelligent than average. Overconfidence bias is investors’ ungrounded belief that their expertise, luck, and/or information are better than they actually are, often ignoring data and statistical probabilities in the process. This phenomenon might be caused by an inadequate understanding of risks and statistical probabilities, optimism in “timing the market”, an illusion that the investor somehow has “control” over the market, an over-optimistic opinion of individuals’ trading experience, or disproportionate confidence in the companies whose stocks are being traded. The bandwagon effect, in which an individual makes an ill-informed trade because many others around them are doing the same, is closely related to the overconfidence bias. 

Overconfidence bias is dangerous because it leads investors to take on more risks than rational and/or make suboptimal investment decisions. To illustrate this further, let us take a look at the GameStop (GME) saga in early 2021. Even after GME increased in value by 1,700% in a few days without a valid reason, many individuals still eagerly bought the stock (the author of this article included) at high prices because there seemed to be a huge wave of people doing the same. They were confident that the price would continue increasing exponentially due to a widespread “theory”. Many traders were first-timers in the stock market. Hence they might have been unaware of the risks they were taking in. Others were confident that their due diligent was correct and decided not to trust mainstream news. Others hoped they would utilize the rising momentum to make the trade of their lifetime. Others were sure that every element in the market would operate exactly as how they envisioned it would – which turned out not to be the case. The GameStop saga saw several stock exchanges around the world severely limiting buying power, hiding stock tickers, and imposing other trading regulations unseen before in recent history. As a result, when the bubble burst a few days later, GME lost around 85% of its 52-week-high value, and many were left with significant losses. 

To avoid overconfidence bias:

  • Do your own careful due diligent using high-quality data sources (e.g., financial statements, news from trustworthy websites). 

  • If you are caught in an eager crowd pooling their money in a particular asset, think critically about the reasons underlying their decision. If you cannot find a valid reason for the investment, do not give in to peer pressure.

  • Apply critical-thinking frameworks to formulate your decision. For example, you can conduct a pre-mortem, which involves listing the reasons why the investment might fail. 

  • Always implement risk management strategies to be prepared in worst-case scenarios.

  • Bear in mind that however confident you are about a decision, market conditions can still change and you can still incur losses. 

  1. Loss aversion

Loss aversion bias makes investors value what they lose more than the same amount that they gain. You might fear losses so much, you stick to low-risk – low-return investments (e.g., only investing in U.S. Treasury bonds) and stay away from higher-risk ones (e.g., investing in the stock market), even when the potential gains are significantly larger. On the other hand, if you are holding a plummeting stock, you might be so afraid to finalize losses that you keep on holding even when there is little chance of the stock recovering. Rationally, sometimes the potential returns of an investment are high enough to justify the calculated added risk. In addition, sometimes it is wise to cut your losses in an investment and use the remaining money on more profitable decisions.

To avoid loss aversion:

  • Do thorough due diligent using high-quality data sources before making an investment.

  • When pondering an investment decision, focus on the potential gains as much as potential losses.

  • Use stop-loss orders to automatically limit your losses when the market condition goes south.

  • Implement risk hedging measures (e.g. buying options, investing in a negatively correlated asset) to minimize the risk of an investment.

  1. Self-serving bias

Self-serving bias is a tendency to attribute successful trades to individual skills and failed ones to bad luck. It is true that sometimes luck plays a significant role in determining the final payout of an investment. Yet, even in an unlucky situation, there are always lessons to be learned to improve the outcome of future trades. While easy on the ego, this bias prevents you from realizing your mistakes, learning from them, and making better investments down the line. 

To avoid self-serving bias:

  • Keep in mind that it is okay to make mistakes and learn from them.

  • Before, during, and after a trade, carefully detail why and how you make your moves.

  • After a trade, whether successful or failed, come back to your notes, and analyze what might have gone right/wrong with the most objective mindset possible. 

  1. Survivorship bias

After deriving a trading strategy, it is reasonable to test your strategy on the historical price of a wide array of stocks to ensure generalizability. Yet, depending on the dataset used, you might still be subject to survivorship bias. 

Survivorship bias happens when you test your strategy only on companies that currently exist (or “survived”) while ignoring those that went bankrupt, delisted, or acquired. Unfortunately, this is often the case if you use freely available datasets, such as those on Yahoo Finance. This observation of only success cases and not failure cases thus makes your final analysis result more optimistic than it should be.

To prevent survivorship bias, make sure you use an inclusive dataset that also contains information about companies that are no longer in the market. 

  1. Impatience

This is not exactly a bias, but still an important emotional factor impacting the quality of our trades. More often than not, we find ourselves buying stocks even before we have thoroughly researched the companies, frantically exiting our position when the stock decreases slightly only to regret the decision when it shoots up again the next day or selling our shares before they reach our price target. Impatience might severely damage your profitability.

To improve on your patience:

  • Take your time doing due diligence to pick appropriate stocks for your portfolio.

  • Be disciplined when trading. Use your research to pre-establish your entry price, target price, and stop-loss price, and try not to deviate from the plan.

  • Wait for the stock to approach your entry price before buying.

  • Remember that it is generally better to spend some time holding a position in the market than trying to time the market. Try not to give in to impulsive emotions.

  • Use stop-loss orders and limit orders to automatically exit your positions once the stock hits your stop-loss/target price.
