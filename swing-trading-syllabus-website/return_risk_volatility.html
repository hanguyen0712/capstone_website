1. What is return?

Returns are the amount of money you gain or lose in an investment. In stock trading, returns are the sum of any dividend paid out to you and the price difference from when you bought the shares to when you sell them, minus any interest you might have paid if you purchased the shares on loan. Returns are often expressed in percentage terms.

    What is risk?

Risk is the potential of losing money when you make an investment. The saying “High risk, high return” means risk and return are highly correlated. High-risk investments require high returns to justify the risk (or else everyone would opt for lower-risk options with similar returns). Each person has a different level of risk tolerance. Age, socioeconomic status, and personality are among the factors contributing to this difference.

Risk is usually measured using historical data. Students can check out a few risk measures in the risk and volatility indicators section.

Regarding whether a risk can be artificially reduced (or hedged) or not, there are two main types of risk, both of which investors encounter: systematic and unsystematic. Systematic risks impact at least a majority of the market and thus are not diversifiable. Examples include the global financial crises, widespread natural disasters, and pandemics. The COVID-19 pandemic that caused a recession in early 2020 is a clear example of a systematic risk since it impacted all industries and all markets. Meanwhile, unsystematic risks specifically affect a company or industry. For example, anti-smoking legislations issued by governments are detrimental to the tobacco industry but not necessarily to the semiconductor or entertainment industry. This type of risk is diversifiable, which means diversifying your investments to different industries, countries, or financial securities helps reduce it. Diversification will be further explained in the risk mitigation section.

We can also categorize a risk based on its cause. For example:

  • Interest rate risk (mostly affects bondholders, since their returns are determined by the interest rate): Decreasing interest rates are good for bondholders hoping to cash out their bonds because now their returns are better than it used to be relative to the market. In contrast, increasing interest rates make bonds lose value relative to the market.

For example, suppose in 2020, you bought a five-year $1,000 bond that yields 3% and hoped to sell it later. The interest rate was 2%. If in 2021, the interest rate increases to 5%, you will have a hard time selling your bond since new bonds with better rates will have already been introduced to the market to compete with your offering. However, if the interest rate decreases to 1%, your bond will be sought after because it offers a higher yield than the interest rate.

  • Inflation risk (affects bondholders): Increased inflation erodes the real (a.k.a. inflation-adjusted) returns of fixed interest rate bonds.

Suppose you bought a one-year $1,000 bond that yields 3% in 2020 and the inflation rate was 2%. Your real rate of return is calculated using the formula [ (1 + nominal yield)/(1 + inflation rate) - 1]. If the inflation rate remains at 2% in 2021, your real rate of return is (1 + 3%)/(1 + 2%) – 1 = 1%. However, if the inflation rate increases to 4% in 2021, your real rate of return is (1 + 3%)/(1 + 4%) – 1 = -1%, which means you incur a loss instead of making a profit. 

  • Volatility risk (mostly affects stockholders): More volatile stocks are considered riskier because their price can plummet quickly and deeply with every piece of unfavorable news. We will talk more about volatility in the next section.

For example, in January 2021, you bought ten shares of Gamestop (GME) for $400 per share. However, a few days later, the stock plummeted to $50 per share, and you decided to exit your position to cut losses. In this case, volatility risk costs you $350 per share or 87.5% of your investment. 

  • Business risk (affects everyone, but stockholders most severely): In the event that a company goes bankrupt, bondholders are the first in line to be paid, while stockholders are the last. If the company runs out of money and asset before paying stockholders, they are paid nothing.

  • Country risk (affects everyone): New regulations, change of leadership, natural disasters, wars, and country-level financial instability are among the factors that impact companies’ performance, market liquidity, and what you can do with your money. 

For example, the COVID-19 pandemic triggered a short recession in 2020 when all stocks slumped (Brradley, 2021). Suppose you bought American Airlines (AAL) shares several years ago at $35 per share. By the time the stock hit $8 per share in 2020, not only had your profit been wiped off, but you had also incurred a 77% loss on your investment. Fortunately, if the country’s risks are short-lived and if companies have strong enough cash flow, operation, and management, the loss on your stock investment may at least be partially reversed as long as you are still holding valid shares. For example, As of March 2021, American Airlines’ stock price has increased to $25 per share (Yahoo Finance, 2021). Hence, instead of losing 77% of your investment, you now only lose (35 – 25)/35 = 28.5%. 

Traders should be familiar with risk management strategies to minimize losses during economic downturns. Students can check out some risk management techniques in the risk mitigation section.

  1. What is volatility?

Volatility is how aggressively the price of financial security moves relative to the market. Volatile securities are considered riskier because predicting their movement is difficult. Just as its price can shoot up with a piece of good news, any bad news from both the company and the market can send it plummeting quickly and deeply. The saying “high risk, high returns” definitely applies here.

Historical volatility is measured using weekly, monthly, or yearly historical data, depending on the time horizon we are interested in. Meanwhile, implied (or projected) volatility tries to predict future price movements based on options pricing. This distinction is due to the fact that a security’s past performance might be different from its future behavior. Since more volatility means more risk, some volatility indicators are also used to measure the riskiness of a security.   1. What is correlation?

Correlation describes how two stocks move relative to each other. A positive correlation means that if stock A moves up, stock B also moves up. A negative correlation means that stock A moves down when stock B moves up. For example, oil stocks (e.g., XOM – Exxon Mobil Corporation) and airline stocks (e.g., UAL – United Airlines) tend to move in opposite directions since increased oil price increases the former profitability while decreasing that of the latter. However, it is worth noting that correlation does not equal causation, as amusingly illustrated in this Harvard Business Review article. 

Figure 4. The negative correlation between oil stock XOM and airline stock UAL.

However, correlation can change depending on changing market conditions or the time frame we pick. For example, the graph above shows that for several years before 2020, XOM and UAL were negatively correlated. However, in the first six months of 2020, the COVID-19 posed a global systemic risk affecting every sector. We thus saw these tickers moving in the same direction, plunging around March 2020 and recovering around June. 

Figure 5. The positive correlation between XOM and UAL.

    This example is poignant proof that correlation does not equate to causation. Just because XOM and UAL move in the same direction does not necessarily mean one stock’s movement depends on the other’s activity. In this case, both stocks are impacted by a confounding factor – the COVID-19 pandemic. Due to travel restrictions, governments halted most forms of transportation. Since people did not get out of their house, the demand for oil and gasoline slumped. Meanwhile, air travel was banned in many places, so airplanes could not fly even if oil were cheap. As a result, both industries suffered. 

The formula to calculate the correlation between stock A and B is:

r=∑(A-A)(B-B)∑A-A2∑B-B2

    Where: 

    r is the correlation coefficient

    A is the price of stock A

    A is the mean price of stock A

B is the price of stock B

    B is the mean price of stock B

    r takes a value in the range [-1, 1]. The closer r is to 1, the more positively correlated the stocks are (e.g., if stock A increases by 10%, stock B also increases by roughly 10%). On the contrary, a value close to -1 means the stocks are negatively correlated (e.g., if stock A increases by 10%, stock B decreases roughly by 10%). A value of 0 means the stocks are uncorrelated to each other (price changes in stock A are not indicative of stock B’s price movements).
